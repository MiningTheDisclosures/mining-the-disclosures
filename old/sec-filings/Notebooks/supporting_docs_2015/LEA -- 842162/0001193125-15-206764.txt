 </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><B><U>CONFLICT MINERALS REPORT </U></B></P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:6pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">Year Ended December&nbsp;31, 2014 </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:18pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><B><U>Section&nbsp;1:
Introduction and Company Overview </U></B></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:6pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">This Conflict Minerals Report (the &#147;Report&#148;) covers the period of January&nbsp;1, 2014 through
December&nbsp;31, 2014 in compliance with Rule 13p-1 (the &#147;Conflict Minerals Rule&#148;) under the Securities and Exchange Act of 1934, as amended (the &#147;Exchange Act&#148;). Under the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform Act (the &#147;Dodd-Frank
Act&#148;), the Securities and Exchange Commission (the &#147;SEC&#148;) issued the Conflict Minerals Rule to require certain companies to disclose their use of conflict minerals if those minerals are &#147;necessary to the functionality or
production of a product&#148; manufactured by those companies. Under the Dodd-Frank Act, those minerals include tin, tungsten, tantalum, or gold (together, such minerals are referred to as &#147;3TG&#148;). Congress enacted the Conflict Minerals
Rule due to concerns that the exploitation and trade of conflict minerals by armed groups is helping to finance conflict in the Democratic Republic of Congo (&#147;DRC&#148;) region. The Conflict Minerals Rule focuses on 3TG emanating from the DRC
and nine adjoining countries (together, the &#147;DRC Covered Countries&#148;). </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">Lear Corporation (&#147;Lear&#148;) is a leading Tier 1 supplier to the
global automotive industry.&nbsp;Our business spans all major automotive markets, and we supply seating and electrical distribution systems and related components to virtually every major automotive manufacturer in the world.&nbsp;As of
December&nbsp;31, 2014, we have manufacturing, engineering and administrative capabilities in 34&nbsp;countries with 219 locations and are continuing to grow our business in all automotive producing regions of the world. </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">Lear is a recognized global leader in complete automotive seat systems and key individual seat component parts. Our seating business consists of the design,
development, engineering, just-in-time assembly and delivery of complete seat systems, as well as the design, development, engineering and manufacture of all major seat components, including seat structures and mechanisms, seat covers and surface
materials such as fabric and leather, seat foam and headrests. We are one of only two independent seat suppliers with global scale and the capability to design, develop, engineer, manufacture and deliver complete seat systems and components in every
major automotive producing region in the world. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">Our electrical business consists of the design, development, engineering and manufacture of complete
electrical distribution systems that route electrical signals and manage electrical power within a vehicle. Key components of our electrical business include wiring harnesses, terminals and connectors, junction boxes, battery chargers, electronic
control modules and wireless control devices. We are one of only four suppliers with complete electrical distribution system and component capabilities in every major automotive producing region in the world. </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">Our products are more fully described in our Annual Report, which can be accessed at: <U>http://lear.com/</U>. </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">Under the Conflict Minerals Rule adopted by the SEC, companies (including Lear) are required to publicly disclose their use of conflict minerals, including
through filing a &#147;conflict minerals report&#148; as an exhibit to Form SD, the form created for conflict minerals disclosure. </P>

 <P STYLE="margin-top:0pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><B><U>Section&nbsp;2: Due Diligence Framework </U></B></P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:6pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">The final Conflict Minerals Rule adopted by the SEC requires that an issuer undertake a due diligence process and such due diligence follow a nationally or
internationally recognized due diligence framework. Lear designed its due diligence measures to be in conformity, in all material respects, with the internationally recognized due diligence framework established by the Organisation for Economic
Co-operation and Development (&#147;OECD&#148;), the Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible Supply Chains of Minerals from Conflict-Affected and High-Risk Areas (the &#147;OECD Guidance&#148;), which satisfies the Conflict Minerals Rule requirements
regarding due diligence. Because Lear&#146;s products, like those of many of our peers in the automotive industry, contain 3TG minerals, Lear also undertook to conduct a &#147;Reasonable Country of Origin Inquiry&#148; (as described in further
detail below) regarding the origin of the 3TG minerals used in its products. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:18pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><B><U>Section&nbsp;3: Due Diligence Measures Undertaken </U></B></P>

Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC) or adjoining countries in the products they manufacture, if the conflict minerals are necessary to the functionality or production of such products. </P>

suppliers to engage in due diligence of their supply chain to understand and report the content of their parts supplied to Lear. </P>
