 <BODY BGCOLOR="WHITE">

 <P STYLE="margin-top:0pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman" ALIGN="right"><B>Exhibit 1.01 </B></P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman" ALIGN="center"><B>Leggett&nbsp;&amp; Platt, Incorporated </B></P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:0pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman" ALIGN="center"><B>Conflict Minerals Report </B></P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:0pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman" ALIGN="center"><B>For the reporting period from January&nbsp;1, 2014 to December&nbsp;31, 2014 </B></P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">This Conflict Minerals Report (the &#147;Report&#148;) of Leggett&nbsp;&amp; Platt, Incorporated (the &#147;Company,&#148; &#147;we,&#148; &#147;our&#148; or
&#147;us&#148;) has been prepared pursuant to Rule 13p-1 and Form SD (collectively the &#147;Rule&#148;) promulgated under the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, as amended, for the reporting period from January&nbsp;1, 2014 to December&nbsp;31, 2014.
</P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">The Rule requires disclosure of certain information when a company manufactures or contracts to manufacture products and the minerals specified in the
Rule are necessary to the functionality or production of those products. These minerals, which we collectively refer to in this Report as &#147;Conflict Minerals,&#148; are columbite-tantalite (coltan), cassiterite, gold, wolframite, or their
derivatives, which are limited to tantalum, tin and tungsten. The &#147;Covered Countries&#148; for purposes of the Rule and this Report are the Democratic Republic of the Congo, Angola, Burundi, Central African Republic, Republic of the Congo,
Rwanda, South Sudan, Tanzania, Uganda, and Zambia. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:18pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><B>The Company </B></P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:6pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">We have over 130 manufacturing facilities located in 19 countries. Our manufacturing facilities are located in Austria, Belgium, Brazil, Canada, China,
Croatia, Denmark, France, Germany, Hungary, India, Italy, Mexico, Poland, South Africa, South Korea, Switzerland, United Kingdom and the United States. Our continuing operations are organized into 18 business units, which are divided into 11
business groups under our four segments: Residential Furnishings; Commercial Products; Industrial Materials; and Specialized Products. Our Store Fixtures business unit is classified as a discontinued operation. </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:18pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><B>The Company&#146;s Reasonable Country of Origin Inquiry </B></P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:6pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><B><U>Reasonable Country of Origin Inquiry</U></B>. We conducted an initial survey of sub-component parts and raw materials we purchased from suppliers. We
assigned authority and responsibility to a person in each business unit to (a)&nbsp;compile an internal inventory of the parts and raw materials that we (i)&nbsp;knew or reasonably suspected contained necessary Conflict Minerals and (ii)&nbsp;knew
or reasonably anticipated would be contained in a product, the manufacture of which is completed in the reporting period, and (b)&nbsp;identify the associated suppliers. Upon completion of the reporting period, we updated our internal inventory and
supplier list. Based on our identified &#147;in-scope&#148; suppliers throughout the process, we conducted a good faith, reasonable country of origin inquiry (&#147;RCOI&#148;) regarding the Conflict Minerals. This RCOI was reasonably designed to
determine whether the Conflict Minerals originated in the Covered Countries and whether the Conflict Minerals came from recycled or scrap sources. We adopted a supplier engagement approach. We sent requests to identified in-scope suppliers for
verification of necessary Conflict Minerals and sourcing information, smelter or refiner (the &#147;SOR&#148;) and mine identification and recycled or scrap source determinations. Requests were sent through the iPoint Conflict Minerals Platform (the
&#147;iPCMP&#148;), a web-based tool designed to communicate and track Conflict Minerals sourcing information throughout the mineral supply chain by use of the <I>Conflict Free Sourcing Initiative</I> (the &#147;CFSI&#148;) <I>Conflict Minerals
Reporting Template</I> (the &#147;CMRT&#148;). Along with the CMRT request, we distributed correspondence to each in-scope supplier which briefly described the requirements of the Rule and contained an electronic link to our Conflict Minerals Policy
(the &#147;Policy&#148;) and our Purchase Order Terms and Conditions (the &#147;Purchase Order Terms&#148;) (each discussed below). </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><B><U>Validation and
Analysis of Supplier Responses</U></B>. The supplier responses on the CMRT were validated, to the extent reasonably practicable, by analyzing each CMRT for the existence of &#147;red flags.&#148; Our &#147;red flags&#148; generally included:
(i)&nbsp;the failure to answer mandatory questions; (ii)&nbsp;the lack of listing at least one SOR for each Conflict Mineral declared; (iii)&nbsp;the inconsistency of the CMRT response with our internal inventory; (iv)&nbsp;the declaration of some
Conflict Minerals originating from Covered Countries and whether named SORs were certified under the <I>CFSI Conflict Free Smelter Program</I> (the &#147;CFSP&#148;); (v)&nbsp;the inclusion of Covered Countries on the SOR page of the CMRT; and
(vi)&nbsp;the CMRT responses conflicting with one another. CMRT information was recorded in our Conflict Minerals Database (the &#147;CM Database&#148;). We prepared and delivered summary reports of supplier responses to senior management in our
Conflict Minerals internal management structure. </P>

<p Style='page-break-before:always'>
<HR  SIZE="3" style="COLOR:#999999" WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">

 <P STYLE="margin-top:0pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><B><U>Follow-up Request to Suppliers</U></B>. Regarding the in-scope suppliers who did not respond, we first sent
reminder requests through the iPCMP, and then if the supplier still did not respond we sent follow-up letters requesting a response. Also, we sent communication requesting the identity of the non-responsive supplier&#146;s supplier in an attempt to
send the CMRT to such &#147;sub-supplier&#148; and to ascertain the identity of the SOR in our mineral supply chain. In the case of suppliers who responded on the CMRT with responses considered to contain &#147;red flags,&#148; as discussed above,
we sent follow-up communications requesting an explanation or elimination of any red flags. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><B><U>Results of Reasonable Country of Origin
Inquiry</U></B>. We identified 392 in-scope suppliers and achieved an 81% response rate with 319 of these suppliers responding. We concluded that the Company has manufactured or contracted to manufacture products which contain Conflict Minerals that
are necessary to their functionality or production. Based on the RCOI, the Company has determined that some of the component parts and raw materials within its products contain necessary Conflict Minerals that originated from recycled or scrap
sources and some of the component parts and raw materials within its products contain necessary Conflict Minerals that originated outside the Covered Countries. These Conflict Minerals were outside the scope of the Company&#146;s due diligence
process described below. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">However, the Company knows or has reason to believe that a portion of its necessary Conflict Minerals originated or may have
originated in the Covered Countries and knows or has reason to believe that those necessary Conflict Minerals may not be solely from recycled or scrap sources. Accordingly, with respect to these Conflict Minerals, the Company conducted due diligence
on the source and chain of custody of such Conflict Minerals. These Conflict Minerals are contained in the &#147;Covered Products&#148; described below. </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:18pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><B>The Company&#146;s Due Diligence Process </B></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:6pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><B><U>Design of
Due Diligence</U></B>. We designed our due diligence measures to conform, in all material respects, to the framework in the <I>Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible Supply Chains of Minerals
from Conflict-Affected and High Risk Areas: Second Edition, </I>including the related supplements on tin, tantalum, tungsten and gold (the &#147;OECD Guidance&#148;). </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><B><U>Scope of Due Diligence</U></B>. Our supply chain regarding the Covered Products is complex, and there are multiple parties in the supply chain between
us and the original sources of Conflict Minerals. In this regard, we did not purchase necessary Conflict Minerals directly from SORs or mines. We must rely on our suppliers to provide information regarding the origin of Conflict Minerals included in
the Covered Products. Moreover, we believe that SORs of the Conflict Minerals are best situated to identify the sources of Conflict Minerals, and we have taken steps attempting to identify the specific SORs of Conflict Minerals in our supply chain.
We conducted due diligence on the source and chain of custody of Conflict Minerals with the objective of determining the origin of the Conflict Minerals contained in the Covered Products, and whether the Conflict Minerals directly or indirectly
financed or benefited the Armed Groups, as defined in the Rule. We began our due diligence in July 2014 and it was substantially complete by mid-May 2015. We made resources available to support the due diligence process. </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">Our due diligence process is based on seeking data from our direct suppliers, and those suppliers seeking similar information within their supply chains to
identify the original sources of the necessary Conflict Minerals. As a downstream purchaser of Conflict Minerals, our due diligence measures can provide only reasonable, not absolute, assurance regarding the source and chain of custody of the
necessary Conflict Minerals. </P>
